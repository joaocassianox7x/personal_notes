Residue Theorem

Summary (Arfken)

Given a function \(f(z)\) that is analytic on and inside a closed contour \(C\) except for isolated poles \(z_k\), the contour integral obeys the residue theorem (Arfken, Weber, Harris, Mathematical Methods for Physicists):

\[\oint_C f(z)\,dz = 2\pi i \sum_k \operatorname{Res}\big(f, z_k\big).\]

Residues capture the coefficient of \((z - z_k)^{-1}\) in the Laurent series of \(f\) about \(z_k\).

Intuition (why it works)

If \(f\) is analytic in a punctured neighborhood of \(z_0\), it has a Laurent expansion

\[f(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} a_n (z-z_0)^n.\]

When you integrate term-by-term around a small circle \(|z-z_0|=\rho\), all powers vanish except the \(n=-1\) term:

\[\begin{split}\oint_{|z-z_0|=\rho} (z-z_0)^n\,dz = \begin{cases} 2\pi i, & n=-1,\\ 0, & n\neq -1. \end{cases}\end{split}\]

So the local contribution of an isolated singularity is exactly \(2\pi i\,a_{-1}\). Summing over all enclosed singularities gives the global contour integral.

Residue formulas

  • Simple pole at \(z_0\): \(\operatorname{Res}(f,z_0) = \lim_{z\to z_0} (z - z_0) f(z)\).

  • Higher-order pole (order \(m\)): \(\operatorname{Res}(f,z_0) = \tfrac{1}{(m-1)!} \lim_{z\to z_0} \tfrac{d^{m-1}}{dz^{m-1}} \big[(z - z_0)^m f(z)\big]\).

  • At infinity (often useful for rational functions): \(\operatorname{Res}(f,\infty)\) equals minus the coefficient of \(z^{-1}\) in the Laurent series of \(f\) at infinity.

Equivalent identities (handy in practice)

For a meromorphic function on the Riemann sphere (e.g., a rational function), the residues satisfy

\[\sum_{k\in\mathbb{C}} \operatorname{Res}(f, z_k) + \operatorname{Res}(f,\infty) = 0.\]

For rational \(f(z)=\tfrac{p(z)}{q(z)}\) with \(\deg q \ge \deg p + 2\), one has \(\operatorname{Res}(f,\infty)=0\), hence the sum of all finite residues is zero.

Fast residue methods (beyond the limit formula)

  • Series match (Laurent/Taylor): expand factors until the \((z-z_0)^{-1}\) term appears.

  • Partial fractions (rational functions): isolate the \(\tfrac{A}{z-z_0}\) term.

  • Simple pole at a simple root of the denominator:

    If \(f(z)=\tfrac{g(z)}{h(z)}\) and \(h(z_0)=0\) with \(h'(z_0)\neq 0\), then

    \[\operatorname{Res}(f,z_0) = \frac{g(z_0)}{h'(z_0)}.\]

  • Logarithmic derivative trick (common in products):

    If \(f(z)=\tfrac{g'(z)}{g(z)}\) and \(g\) has a simple zero at \(z_0\), then \(\operatorname{Res}(f,z_0)=1\).

Practical checklist

  1. Choose contour: match decay/symmetry of the integrand (unit circle for trigonometric, semicircle for decaying exponentials, keyholes for branch cuts).

  2. Rewrite integrand: express in \(z\) with \(z = e^{ix}\) or other substitutions; convert differentials (e.g., \(dx = \tfrac{dz}{i z}\)).

  3. Locate poles inside contour: classify orders and check if any cancel.

  4. Compute residues: use limit formulas for simple poles or quick partial fractions when possible.

  5. Sum residues and multiply by :math:`2pi i`: then take real/imag part if needed for the original real integral.

Notes on contour choices

  • Unit circle: trigonometric integrals via \(z=e^{ix}\).

  • Upper/lower semicircle: Fourier-type integrals with exponential decay in half-planes.

  • Keyhole: branch cuts on the positive real axis for logarithms or fractional powers.

  • Indented contours: avoid poles on the real axis by small semicircular detours.

Methodology

  1. Choose a contour that encloses the singularities relevant to the real integral (often the unit circle for trigonometric integrals using \(z = e^{ix}\)).

  2. Rewrite the integrand as a complex function \(f(z)\) and express \(dx\) in terms of \(dz\).

  3. Identify poles inside the contour and compute their residues (by series expansion or the limit formula for simple poles).

  4. Apply \(\oint_C f(z) dz = 2\pi i \sum \operatorname{Res}(f, z_k)\) and take the real part if the original integral was real-valued.

Worked examples

Example 1: \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{dx}{x^2+a^2}\)

Assume \(a>0\). Consider the semicircular contour in the upper half-plane: the real segment from \(-R\) to \(R\) plus the arc \(z=Re^{i\theta}\), \(\theta\in[0,\pi]\).

Let

\[f(z) = \frac{1}{z^2+a^2} = \frac{1}{(z-ia)(z+ia)}.\]

There is a single pole inside the contour at \(z=ia\). Its residue is

\[\operatorname{Res}(f, ia) = \lim_{z\to ia} (z-ia)\frac{1}{(z-ia)(z+ia)} = \frac{1}{2ia}.\]

Hence

\[\oint_C f(z)\,dz = 2\pi i\,\frac{1}{2ia} = \frac{\pi}{a}.\]

On the arc, \(|f(z)|\le \tfrac{1}{R^2-a^2}\) so the arc contribution vanishes as \(R\to\infty\). Therefore

\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{dx}{x^2+a^2} = \frac{\pi}{a}.\]

Example 2: \(\int_0^{\infty} \frac{\cos(bx)}{x^2+a^2}\,dx\)

Assume \(a>0\) and first take \(b>0\). Consider

\[I(b)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{e^{ibx}}{x^2+a^2}\,dx.\]

Close the contour in the upper half-plane. By Jordan’s lemma the arc integral vanishes (because \(e^{ibz}=e^{ibx-b\,\Im z}\) decays when \(\Im z>0\)). The only enclosed pole is \(z=ia\) with residue

\[\operatorname{Res}\left(\frac{e^{ibz}}{z^2+a^2}, ia\right) = \frac{e^{ib(ia)}}{2ia} = \frac{e^{-ab}}{2ia}.\]

Therefore

\[I(b)=2\pi i\,\frac{e^{-ab}}{2ia}=\frac{\pi}{a}e^{-ab}.\]

Taking the real part gives

\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\cos(bx)}{x^2+a^2}\,dx = \frac{\pi}{a}e^{-ab}.\]

Since the integrand is even,

\[\int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{\cos(bx)}{x^2+a^2}\,dx = \frac{\pi}{2a}e^{-ab}\qquad (b>0).\]

For general real \(b\), symmetry yields \(e^{-ab}\to e^{-a|b|}\).

Example 3: \(\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{d\theta}{a+b\cos\theta}\)

Assume \(a>|b|>0\) so the denominator never vanishes on the real interval.

Set \(z=e^{i\theta}\) so \(d\theta = \tfrac{dz}{iz}\) and \(\cos\theta=\tfrac{1}{2}\left(z+z^{-1}\right)\). Then

\[\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{d\theta}{a+b\cos\theta} = \oint_{|z|=1} \frac{1}{a+\tfrac{b}{2}(z+z^{-1})}\,\frac{dz}{iz} = \oint_{|z|=1} \frac{2}{i\,(b z^2 + 2 a z + b)}\,dz.\]

The poles are the roots of \(b z^2 + 2 a z + b=0\):

\[z_{\pm} = \frac{-a \pm \sqrt{a^2-b^2}}{b}.\]

For \(a>|b|\), one root lies inside the unit circle and the other outside; the inside pole is

\[z_- = \frac{-a + \sqrt{a^2-b^2}}{b},\qquad |z_-|<1.\]

Since it is a simple pole, using \(\operatorname{Res}\big(\tfrac{1}{P(z)},z_-\big)=\tfrac{1}{P'(z_-)}\) for \(P(z)=b z^2 +2az + b\),

\[\operatorname{Res}\left(\frac{2}{i\,P(z)}, z_-\right) = \frac{2}{i\,P'(z_-)} = \frac{2}{i\,(2b z_- + 2a)}.\]

But \(2b z_- + 2a = 2\sqrt{a^2-b^2}\). Therefore

\[\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{d\theta}{a+b\cos\theta} = 2\pi i\,\frac{2}{i\,2\sqrt{a^2-b^2}} = \frac{2\pi}{\sqrt{a^2-b^2}}.\]

Example 4: \(\int_0^{2\pi} \cos^{2n}(x)\,dx\)

Let \(z = e^{ix}\) so \(\cos x = \tfrac{1}{2}(z + z^{-1})\) and \(dx = \tfrac{dz}{i z}\) on the unit circle \(|z| = 1\).

\[I_n = \int_0^{2\pi} \cos^{2n}(x)\,dx = \Re\left( \oint_{|z|=1} \frac{1}{i z} \left( \frac{z + z^{-1}}{2} \right)^{2n} dz \right).\]

Expand the binomial \((z + z^{-1})^{2n} = \sum_{k=0}^{2n} \binom{2n}{k} z^{2k-2n}\). Then

\[\frac{1}{i z} \left( \frac{z + z^{-1}}{2} \right)^{2n} = \frac{1}{i 2^{2n}} \sum_{k=0}^{2n} \binom{2n}{k} z^{2k-2n-1}.\]

The only term contributing a residue at \(z = 0\) is the \(z^{-1}\) term, which occurs when \(2k - 2n - 1 = -1\), i.e., \(k = n\). Hence

\[\operatorname{Res}\left( \frac{1}{i z} \left( \frac{z + z^{-1}}{2} \right)^{2n}, 0 \right) = \frac{1}{i 2^{2n}} \binom{2n}{n}.\]

Applying the residue theorem yields

\[I_n = 2\pi i \cdot \frac{1}{i 2^{2n}} \binom{2n}{n} = \frac{2\pi}{2^{2n}} \binom{2n}{n} = 2\pi \frac{(2n)!}{4^{n} (n!)^2}.\]

This matches the standard reduction formula and provides a quick contour-integral evaluation for even powers of cosine.

Example 5 (keyhole/branch cut): \(\int_0^{\infty} \frac{x^{\alpha-1}}{1+x}\,dx\)

This classical contour integral connects residues to the Beta/Gamma functions. Assume \(0<\alpha<1\) and define the branch

\[z^{\alpha-1} = e^{(\alpha-1)\Log z},\qquad \Arg z\in(0,2\pi).\]

Use a keyhole contour encircling the positive real axis (with a small circle around the origin and a large circle at infinity). Consider

\[f(z)=\frac{z^{\alpha-1}}{1+z}.\]

There is a simple pole at \(z=-1\) with residue

\[\operatorname{Res}(f,-1)=\lim_{z\to-1}(z+1)\frac{z^{\alpha-1}}{1+z}=(-1)^{\alpha-1}=e^{i\pi(\alpha-1)}.\]

The integral over the two sides of the cut differ by a phase. On the upper side \(\Arg z = 0\), so \(z^{\alpha-1}=x^{\alpha-1}\); on the lower side \(\Arg z = 2\pi\), so \(z^{\alpha-1}=e^{2\pi i(\alpha-1)}x^{\alpha-1}\). Taking the contour orientation into account, one obtains

\[\oint_C f(z)\,dz = \left(1-e^{2\pi i(\alpha-1)}\right)\int_0^{\infty}\frac{x^{\alpha-1}}{1+x}\,dx = \left(1-e^{2\pi i\alpha}\right)\int_0^{\infty}\frac{x^{\alpha-1}}{1+x}\,dx.\]

By residues, the same contour integral equals

\[\oint_C f(z)\,dz = 2\pi i\,\operatorname{Res}(f,-1)=2\pi i\,e^{i\pi(\alpha-1)}.\]

Using \(1-e^{2\pi i\alpha} = -2i e^{i\pi\alpha}\sin(\pi\alpha)\), it follows that

\[\int_0^{\infty}\frac{x^{\alpha-1}}{1+x}\,dx = \frac{\pi}{\sin(\pi\alpha)}\qquad (0<\alpha<1).\]

Example 6 (pole on the integration path): \(\mathrm{PV}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\sin x}{x}\,dx\)

This illustrates indented contours and principal values. Consider

\[J = \mathrm{PV}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{e^{ix}}{x}\,dx.\]

Close in the upper half-plane and detour around the pole at the origin with a small semicircle of radius \(\varepsilon\) above the real axis. There are no poles in the upper half-plane, but the indentation contributes

\[\int_{\text{indent}} \frac{e^{iz}}{z}\,dz \xrightarrow[\varepsilon\to 0]{} i\pi.\]

Hence \(J=i\pi\). Taking the imaginary part gives the standard result

\[\mathrm{PV}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\sin x}{x}\,dx = \pi.\]

Additional examples to practice

  • Compute residues for higher-order poles, e.g. \(\operatorname{Res}\big(\tfrac{e^z}{z^3},0\big)\) via series.

  • Evaluate \(\int_0^{2\pi} \tfrac{d\theta}{5-4\cos\theta}\) and check it matches \(\tfrac{2\pi}{\sqrt{5^2-4^2}}\).

  • Use \(\operatorname{Res}(f,\infty)\) to verify that the sum of all residues of a rational function is zero.
